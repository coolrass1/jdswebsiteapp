SRA Transparency and Complaints Compliance: A Comprehensive Guide for Law Firms
1. Introduction to the SRA Transparency Framework
The SRA Transparency Rules, which came into force in December 2018, mandate that authorised firms and individual solicitors provide clear, accurate, and accessible information to the public. These requirements are designed to enable consumers and small businesses to make informed choices, fostering competition and improving public trust in the legal sector.
Core Objectives

    Informed Choice: Ensuring potential clients understand price and service levels before instructing a firm.
    Market Competition: Improving the ability of consumers to compare different legal service providers.
    Public Confidence: Strengthening the reputation of the profession through regulatory openness.
    Accountability: Standardising how complaints procedures and regulatory statuses are disclosed.

2. Rule 1: Price and Service Transparency
Authorised bodies must publish cost and service information if they offer any of the following services to the public or businesses. This information must be prominent and easy for users to find on the firm’s website.

Mandatory Cost Requirements (SRA Rule 1.5)
Per Rule 1.5, cost information must precisely include:

    a) Total Cost: The total price of the service or, where not practicable, the average cost or range of costs based on a typical case.
    b) Basis for Charges: Clarity on the charging structure, including all hourly rates or fixed fees.
    c) Staff Qualifications: The experience and qualifications of anyone carrying out the work and their supervisors.
    d) Payments to Third Parties (Disbursements): A description and cost for each likely disbursement. If the actual cost is unknown, an average or range must be provided.
    e) VAT: Confirmation of whether fees or disbursements attract VAT, and the specific amount/rate of VAT. Firms not VAT-registered must explicitly state this.
    f) Service Elements: Details of what is included in the price, key stages, likely timescales for each stage, and any services that a client might reasonably expect to be included but are excluded.
    g) CFA/DBA Details: If using conditional fee or damages-based agreements, the specific circumstances in which a client may have to make payments themselves.

3. Specific Pricing Models and Templates
Compliance requires more than just listing a price; it requires synthesizing "Good Practice" into the user experience.
Pricing Model Logic

    Typical Case Approach: When providing averages, firms should use a "typical case" model rather than a strict mathematical mean, as this is often more representative for consumers.
    Range of Costs: If providing a range, you must list the factors that determine whether a case falls at the lower or higher end (e.g., complexity, urgency).
    Immigration VAT Nuance: Charging VAT in immigration depends on the client’s location and status. Firms must include this statement: "We will confirm whether VAT is payable once we have been instructed and can determine if it is properly chargeable."

Template Structure: Conveyancing and Probate
Firms are encouraged to use plain English. Replace the term "Disbursements" with "Payments to Third Parties" where possible.
Service Stage Example: Residential Purchase

    Instruction: Reviewing finances and checking title documents.
    Due Diligence: Carrying out searches and raising enquiries with the seller's solicitor.
    Contract Exchange: Finalising contracts and moving toward the completion date.
    Completion: Managing the transfer of funds and finalising the purchase.
    Post-Completion: Paying Stamp Duty and registering the title at the Land Registry.
    Our Fees: Legal fee + VAT + electronic money transfer fees.
    Payments to Third Parties (Disbursements): Search fees, HM Land Registry fee + VAT (where applicable).
    Tax: Stamp Duty Land Tax (England) or Land Transaction Tax (Wales). Provide links to HMRC/WRA calculators rather than fixed estimates.

4. Rule 2: Mandatory Complaints Procedure
All firms must publish their complaints handling procedure. If a firm does not have a website, it must provide this information in writing immediately upon request.
Procedure Requirements

    Clarity: Use plain language to describe how to complain and to whom.
    Timelines: State that you will acknowledge complaints (e.g., within 3 working days) and provide a target resolution date (typically 28 days or 8 weeks maximum).
    Protection: State that making a complaint will not affect how the client's case is handled.

Mandatory Escalation Text (Updated April 2023) Firms must provide the following details regarding the Legal Ombudsman and the SRA:
Legal Ombudsman: If we are unable to resolve your complaint, you can contact the Legal Ombudsman. You must take your complaint to them:

    Within six months of receiving our final response; and
    No more than one year from the date of the act or omission being complained about; or
    No more than one year from the date when you should reasonably have known that there was cause for complaint.

Solicitors Regulation Authority: The SRA can help if you are concerned about our behaviour, such as dishonesty, taking or losing your money, or treating you unfairly. Visit the SRA website to see how you can raise a concern.

5. Rule 4: Regulatory Disclosures and the SRA Digital Badge
Firms must demonstrate their authorised status through two primary digital requirements.
The SRA Digital Badge
Mandatory since November 2019, the digital badge verifies authorization by linking to the SRA register.

    Provider: The badge is administered by Yoshki.
    Access: Only an Authorized Signatory or an Organisation Contact within the firm can register the domain in mySRA.

Activation Steps:

    Log in to mySRA.
    Navigate to My Organisations > Organisation Name > Office Details.
    Select Edit to enter the correct website URL and save.
    Wait 24 hours for the badge to activate via the Yoshki script.

Correspondence and Website Standards

    Website: Both the SRA Number and the Digital Badge must be displayed in a prominent place (usually the footer).
    Letterheads & Emails: Must show the SRA number and the exact phrase: "authorised and regulated by the Solicitors Regulation Authority."

6. Publication and Accessibility (Rule 3)
Regulatory information must be clear and easy for a layperson to find. It cannot be hidden.
[RED TEXT] Common Compliance Pitfalls:

    [RED TEXT] "Fees on Request": This phrase is a direct violation for any service in scope.
    [RED TEXT] Using Downloadable PDFs: Information must be on the webpage itself. Downloadable files do not meet the "accessible" requirement.
    [RED TEXT] Requiring Personal Data: You cannot force a user to provide an email or phone number before showing prices.
    [RED TEXT] Missing Supervisor Details: Listing a fee earner without their supervisor’s name and qualifications is a common omission.

Best Practice: Use "Settling in the UK" instead of "Indefinite Leave to Remain" to ensure accessibility for non-native speakers. Perform a compliance audit every six months.

8. Compliance Checklist for Firms
Use this list for an immediate executive audit of your firm's compliance:

    [ ] Rule 1 Scope Audit: Do all in-scope service pages (Conveyancing, Probate, etc.) exist and contain pricing?
    [ ] Rule 1.5 Mapping: Does each price page contain all seven elements (a-g), including staff qualifications and supervisors?
    [ ] Plain English Review: Is the term "Payments to third parties" used in place of "Disbursements"?
    [ ] VAT Status: Is the firm's VAT registration status and rate (or lack thereof) explicitly stated on every pricing page?
    [ ] Immigration VAT: Does the immigration section include the specific nuance regarding VAT-payable checks?
    [ ] Complaints Procedure: Does the website link directly to a procedure containing the April 2023 Ombudsman timeframes?
    [ ] Digital Badge & SRA Number: Are both the Yoshki badge and the firm's SRA number prominently displayed on the website?
    [ ] Correspondence Check: Are all email signatures and letterheads updated with the mandatory "authorised and regulated" wording?
    [ ] Authorized Signatory Check: Has the website domain been verified in mySRA by a person with the correct permissions?
    [ ] Accessibility: Is all information available directly on the page without requiring a PDF download or contact form?
